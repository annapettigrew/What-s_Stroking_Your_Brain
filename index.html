QUIZ: What's Your Stroke Risk?

A stroke is a medical condition in which poor blood flow to the brain causes cell death. Every 40 seconds someone in the United States suffers from a stroke. According to the World Health Organization (WHO) stroke is the 2nd leading cause of death globally, responsible for approximately 11% of total deaths.

There are two main types of stroke: ischemic, due to lack of blood flow, and hemorrhagic, due to bleeding. Both cause parts of the brain to stop functioning properly. While numerous studies have tried/trying to figure out what triggers strokes amoung various individuals, there are a few modifiable and non-modifiable risk that increase your chances.

We sought out to predict whether a patient is likely to have a stroke based on the input parameters like gender, age, various disease, residence type, work type, age, smoking status, and marital status.

Stroke Risk: Features of Importance

40+

Age

Our data shows that the older you are, the more likely you are to have a stroke. The chance of having a stroke about doubles every 10 years after age 55. Although stroke is common among older adults, many people younger than 65 years also have strokes. CDC.gov

>126

Avg Glucose Level

A person with an average glucose level greater than 126 mg/dl is diagnosed with diabetes. Our patients, who are risk for stroke, has an average blood gluocse level of 152 mg/dl. Diabetes increases your risk for stroke, which causes sugars to build up in the blood and prevent oxygen and nutrients from getting to the various parts of your body, including your brain. CDC.gov

30+

Body Mass Index (BMI)

Obesity is excess body fat or body mass index (BMI) greater than 25. Obesity is linked to higher “bad” cholesterol and triglyceride levels and to lower “good” cholesterol levels. Obesity can also lead to high blood pressure and diabetes. CDC.gov

Random Forest (Feature Importance)

The feature importance (variable importance) describes which features are relevant. It can help with better understanding of the solved problem and sometimes lead to model improvements by employing the feature selection. Feature importance is calculated as the decrease in node impurity weighted by the probability of reaching that node. The node probability can be calculated by the number of samples that reach the node, divided by the total number of samples. The higher the value the more important the feature.

Starting at 25.0, the higher your BMI, the greater is your risk of developing obesity-related health problems. These ranges of BMI are used to describe levels of risk:

  • Overweight (not obese), if BMI is 25.0 to 29.9
  • Class 1 (low-risk) obesity, if BMI is 30.0 to 34.9
  • Class 2 (moderate-risk) obesity, if BMI is 35.0 to 39.9
  • Class 3 (high-risk) obesity, if BMI is equal to or greater than 40.0

Visualization: Stroke Risk Features

The chart show features, age, bmi, and avg glucose lvl, that are binned together for stroke risk. Age bin is greater and less than 40. BMI bin are healthy weight, obesity, overweight, and underweight according to CDC.gov. And, average glucose level bins are grouped as Low ( less than 70 mg/dL), Normal (70 -100 mg/dL), Pre-diabetes (100-125 mg/dL), and diabetes (>126 mg/dL).

As noted earlier, as body mass index increase (bmi), you become a greater risk of having diabetes. Diabetes, according to NIH, causes various microvascular and macrovascular changes often culminating in major clinical complications, 1 of which, is stroke. Individuals with diabetes are especially susceptible to the consequenes of cerebral small vessel diseases, which is linked to stroke as well.

Out of the top 3 risk factors, age, bmi, and average blood gluocse, two (bmi and average blood glucose) are modifiable. Modify risk factors in the chart to see how the count of strokes increase or decrease.
